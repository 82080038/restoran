# Menu Engineering & Pricing Strategy

## Fundamentals of Menu Engineering

### Definition
Menu engineering is the strategic approach to designing and pricing a restaurant's menu with the goal of maximizing profitability. It involves analyzing the performance of each menu item in terms of both popularity and profitability, then using this data to optimize menu layout, pricing, and promotion.

### Core Metrics

#### Plate Cost
- **Definition**: What one specific dish costs to make once, ingredient by ingredient
- **Calculation**: Take the recipe, list every ingredient at its edible-portion cost, multiply by quantity used, sum, divide by batch yield
- **Target**: Roughly three times plate cost for full-service independents (28-33% food cost range)
- **Importance**: This is the floor the menu price must clear

#### Food Cost Percentage
- **Definition**: The kitchen's ingredient spend across a period as a share of sales
- **Formula**: (Cost of Ingredients ÷ Selling Price) × 100
- **Target Range**: 28-35% for full-service restaurants
- **Variations**: 
  - Bars and pizzerias: 15-25%
  - Steakhouses or seafood-led venues: 40%+
- **Note**: The percentage matters less than absolute contribution margin per cover

#### Contribution Margin
- **Definition**: Sell price minus food cost
- **Importance**: The euro/dollar figure each plate puts toward overheads
- **Comparison**: A 35% food-cost item that contributes $15 per plate is almost always better than a 22% food-cost item that contributes $4

### The Four-Quadrant Framework

#### Stars (High Popularity, High Profitability)
- **Characteristics**: Best-selling items with strong margins
- **Strategy**: 
  - Prominently display on menu
  - Use as anchors for pricing
  - Maintain quality and consistency
  - Consider slight price increases
- **Placement**: Prime menu real estate (center-right, top of lists)

#### Plowhorses (High Popularity, Low Profitability)
- **Characteristics**: High volume but thin margins
- **Strategy**:
  - Re-engineer food cost (switch suppliers, change ingredients)
  - Small, frequent price increases
  - Support with value-added improvements
  - Avoid drastic price jumps
- **Example**: Switch bun supplier, drop imported gherkin for local, change side salad blend

#### Puzzles (Low Popularity, High Profitability)
- **Characteristics**: Strong margins but poor sales
- **Strategy**:
  - Promote through specials
  - Improve menu item itself
  - Enhance ingredients and plating
  - Reposition on menu with better visibility
  - Consider price reduction to drive volume

#### Dogs (Low Popularity, Low Profitability)
- **Characteristics**: Poor performance on both metrics
- **Strategy**:
  - Remove from menu
  - Completely redesign
  - Re-purpose as special
  - Replace with new item

## Pricing Strategies

### Cost-Plus Pricing
- **Formula**: Plate price = plate food cost ÷ target food cost percentage
- **Example**: Salmon plate costs $5.60 in ingredients, 28% target food cost = $20.00 floor price
- **Advantage**: Simple, disciplined, ensures margin targets
- **Limitation**: Doesn't consider market competition or perceived value

### Value-Based Pricing
- **Approach**: Price based on customer perception of value
- **Factors**: Quality, uniqueness, dining experience, brand positioning
- **Implementation**: Market research, competitive analysis, customer feedback
- **Challenge**: Requires deep understanding of customer psychology

### Competition-Based Pricing
- **Approach**: Price relative to competitors
- **Methods**: 
  - Parity pricing (match competitors)
  - Premium pricing (above competitors)
  - Penetration pricing (below competitors)
- **Risk**: Can lead to price wars and margin erosion

### Psychological Pricing
- **Charm Pricing**: Prices ending in .95 or .99 feel significantly lower
  - $14.95 reads as "in the $14 range"
  - $15.00 reads as "fifteen dollars"
  - Works well at casual price points
- **Round Pricing**: Round numbers ($16, $24, $38) signal quality and intent
  - Outperforms charm pricing at fine-dining price points
  - Conveys confidence and premium positioning
- **Anchoring**: Place high-priced item at top of category
  - $65 seafood tower makes $38 salmon seem like a bargain
  - Helps guests calibrate value

### Tiered Pricing
- **Three-Tier Structure**: Small ($8), Medium ($12), Large ($13)
- **Psychology**: Large suddenly looks like exceptional value
- **Result**: Most guests upgrade from medium to large
- **Benefit**: Increases both revenue and satisfaction

## Menu Design Principles

### Visual Hierarchy
- **Golden Triangle**: Top center, top right, center right receive most attention
- **Prime Placement**: 
  - High-profit items in eye-catching positions
  - First and last items in categories get 25% more attention
  - Center placement increases visibility
- **Whitespace**: Don't overcrowd; use space to guide attention
- **Imagery**: Strategic photos can increase sales by 30%

### Menu Layout Optimization
- **Category Order**: Start with high-margin categories
- **Item Positioning**: 
  - Place stars first or last in categories
  - Use boxes or borders to highlight items
  - Separate high-margin items with visual dividers
- **Description Length**: Longer descriptions can justify higher prices
- **Pricing Placement**: 
  - Downplay pricing (no dollar signs, lighter font)
  - Right-align prices to reduce comparison
  - Integrate prices into descriptions

### Menu Engineering Process
1. **Pull Sales Data**: Item counts from POS for 30-90 days
2. **Calculate Food Cost**: Raw ingredient cost at current prices
3. **Compute Contribution Margin**: Selling Price − Food Cost
4. **Classify Items**: Assign to quadrants based on popularity and profitability
5. **Develop Strategy**: Create action plan for each quadrant
6. **Implement Changes**: Adjust menu, pricing, and placement
7. **Monitor Results**: Track performance changes
8. **Iterate**: Run quarterly to identify new opportunities

## Advanced Menu Engineering Techniques

### Recipe Costing Best Practices
- **Edible Portion Cost**: Cost after trimming and waste
- **Yield Correction**: Account for prep loss (8% oil loss in frying)
- **Garnish Inclusion**: Include all garnishes and accompaniments
- **Supplier Price vs Received Cost**: Cost from actual inventory, not price lists
- **Batch Recipe Costing**: Divide total batch cost by yield for portion cost

### Menu Psychology
- **Decoy Effect**: Add an expensive option to make others seem reasonable
- **Loss Aversion**: Frame discounts as avoiding loss rather than gaining
- **Social Proof**: Indicate popularity ("Customer favorite")
- **Scarcity**: Limited-time offers create urgency
- **Choice Architecture**: Guide decisions through menu structure

### Seasonal Menu Rotation
- **Benefits**: 
  - Takes advantage of seasonal pricing
  - Keeps menu fresh and exciting
  - Reduces food cost through seasonal ingredients
  - Creates marketing opportunities
- **Implementation**: 
  - Plan quarterly rotations
  - Maintain core signature items
  - Test seasonal items as specials first
  - Communicate changes to staff

### Dynamic Pricing
- **Demand-Based Pricing**: Adjust based on time, day, season
- **Inventory-Based Pricing**: Promote items approaching expiration
- **Event-Based Pricing**: Premium pricing during high-demand periods
- **Channel-Based Pricing**: Different prices for dine-in vs delivery
- **Technology**: AI systems analyze demand patterns and recommend adjustments

## Common Menu Engineering Mistakes

### Costing Errors
- **Undercounting Plate Cost**: Most operators underestimate by 10-15%
- **Ignoring Garnishes**: Forgetting sauces, sides, and accompaniments
- **Wrong Unit Conversions**: Ordering by case, storing by pound, recipe by ounce
- **Yield Neglect**: Not accounting for prep loss and trim
- **Outdated Prices**: Using old supplier prices instead of current costs

### Strategic Errors
- **Focusing Only on Food Cost %**: Ignoring absolute contribution margin
- **Uniform Price Increases**: Applying flat percentage across entire menu
- **Ignoring Value Perception**: Pricing without considering customer perception
- **Over-Engineering**: Making menu too complex
- **Insufficient Testing**: Not testing changes before full rollout

### Design Errors
- **Poor Visual Hierarchy**: Not guiding eye movement effectively
- **Overcrowding**: Too many items overwhelming customers
- **Inconsistent Pricing**: Mixing pricing conventions (.95 and round numbers)
- **Weak Descriptions**: Not selling the item through copy
- **Poor Organization**: Illogical category structure

## Industry Benchmarks

### Food Cost Targets by Concept
- **Fine Dining**: 30-38%
- **Full-Service Casual**: 28-35%
- **Fast Casual**: 25-32%
- **Quick Service**: 25-30%
- **Bars/Pubs**: 15-25%
- **Pizzerias**: 20-28%

### Menu Size Guidelines
- **Fine Dining**: 8-12 items per category
- **Full-Service**: 10-15 items per category
- **Fast Casual**: 12-20 items per category
- **Quick Service**: 15-25 items per category

### Price Point Ranges
- **Appetizers**: $8-18 (casual), $12-28 (fine dining)
- **Entrées**: $15-35 (casual), $28-65 (fine dining)
- **Desserts**: $6-12 (casual), $10-18 (fine dining)
- **Beverages**: $3-8 (non-alcoholic), $8-16 (alcoholic)

## Conclusion

Menu engineering is both art and science—requiring accurate data, strategic thinking, and creative execution. The most successful operators treat their menu as a portfolio of investments, continuously analyzing performance and making data-driven decisions to optimize profitability. When done well, menu engineering can increase gross profit per cover by 10-15% without raising prices or compromising quality.

The key is to move beyond simple cost-plus pricing to a comprehensive approach that considers customer psychology, competitive dynamics, and operational realities. Regular quarterly reviews ensure the menu remains aligned with changing costs, customer preferences, and market conditions.
